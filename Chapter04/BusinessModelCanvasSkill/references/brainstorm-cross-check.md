# Brainstorm Cross-Check

When the canvas comes out of a brainstorming or ideation session, the canvas is
a **synthesis** and the session output is the **source**. This file is the
method for reading that source (usually an image) and checking the canvas
against it. It complements the internal-consistency checks in the other two
reference files; use it only when a session artifact is provided.

Why this matters: synthesis is lossy. Good ideas get dropped, disagreements get
smoothed into a single clean answer, and the write-up quietly adds claims the
room never actually made. The brainstorming research calls the underlying risk
*premature convergence / fixation* — teams lock onto early or loud ideas and
stop exploring — and *evaluation apprehension*, where dissent goes unspoken and
then invisible. A canvas can look finished precisely because it has buried these.

---

## A. Read the artifact (without inventing anything)

**Identify the medium first**, because each carries structure differently:

- **Whiteboard / flipchart photo** — free text, often with headers, arrows,
  boxes, and emphasis (underlines, circles, ALL CAPS, "!!!").
- **Sticky notes / affinity clusters** — each note is one idea; *clusters* and
  their headings carry meaning; color often encodes a category or author.
- **Mind map** — center = core concept; branches = sub-themes; depth = how far
  an idea was developed.
- **Dot-vote / matrix** — dots, stars, or checkmarks signal what the room
  *valued*; position on a 2×2 (e.g. impact × effort) signals prioritization.
- **Digital board (Miro/Mural) screenshot** — same as sticky notes, plus
  possible frames, connectors, and comment threads.

**Extract, then map.** Transcribe items into a quick inventory and tag each:

| Signal on the board | What it usually means | How to treat it |
|---|---|---|
| Dots / stars / "+1" | The room valued this | A dropped high-vote idea is a strong finding |
| Circled / "DECIDED" | An explicit decision | Canvas should reflect it; if not, flag |
| Crossed out / "parked" | Deliberately set aside | *Not* a dropped idea — note it was rejected |
| "?" / question note | Open question | Canvas shouldn't present it as resolved |
| Competing notes / "vs" | Live disagreement | Watch for false consensus in the canvas |
| Arrow between notes | A dependency or causal claim | Check the canvas preserves the logic |
| Color grouping | A category or owner | Helps map items to blocks |

Then map each extracted item to one of the nine blocks, or to "unmapped / meta"
(process notes, agenda, jokes, parking lot).

**The no-fabrication rule is absolute.** If you cannot read something, mark it
*illegible* and exclude it from the comparison — never guess at an idea and then
fault the canvas for omitting your guess. If much of the board is unreadable or
the stakes are high, list back what you *did* extract and ask the person to
confirm or fill gaps before you critique. A confident critique built on a
misread sticky note destroys trust faster than any missed finding.

---

## B. The canvas-vs-brainstorm finding catalogue

Run these comparisons. Lead with whichever produces the most consequential
finding, not all of them mechanically.

1. **Dropped ideas (orphans).** Items on the board — *especially voted or
   starred ones* — that appear in no block. Ask: was this a deliberate cut or an
   accidental loss? An orphaned high-vote idea is often the single best finding.
2. **Unsupported blocks (inventions).** Canvas content with no trace on the
   board. Maybe it's sound outside knowledge — or maybe it was added during
   write-up and never pressure-tested by the room. Flag it as an assumption to
   confirm, not a settled fact.
3. **False consensus.** The board shows competing options, a "vs", or unresolved
   "?" notes; the canvas presents one tidy answer as if agreed. Surface the
   suppressed alternative and ask whether it was actually decided or just
   quietly dropped.
4. **Premature convergence / fixation.** The session diverged widely but the
   canvas centers on the first or loudest idea, with richer or more distant
   alternatives left un-pursued. Steelman the strongest discarded option and ask
   why it lost — if there's no good answer, that's the finding.
5. **Mis-sorting.** An idea landed in the wrong block (a value-prop idea filed
   as a customer segment, a channel filed as a partnership). Mis-sorting often
   hides a deeper confusion about what the idea actually is.
6. **Energy vs. evidence mismatch.** The most-voted / highest-energy idea on the
   board is *not* what the canvas builds around. Either the canvas is ignoring
   the room's conviction, or the room's enthusiasm wasn't backed by evidence —
   worth naming which.
7. **Unresolved questions treated as resolved.** Parking-lot items and open "?"
   notes that the canvas silently answers. Pull them back onto the table as
   things still to test.
8. **Risks raised, not carried forward.** Concerns or objections voiced in the
   session (often the angry-red notes) that don't show up anywhere as
   assumptions to test. The room already did the hard part of spotting them;
   don't let the canvas lose them.

## C. Keep the stance

Everything in the base skill still holds — respectful, open, selective, and
constructive. Two cross-check-specific notes:

- **Point to the evidence.** "The board's top-voted note, *'pay-per-use
  pricing'*, isn't reflected in Revenue Streams — was that a deliberate cut?"
  lands far better than "you ignored the brainstorm." Cite what you saw.
- **A dropped idea is a question, not an accusation.** Teams cut ideas for good
  reasons you can't see from a photo. Offer the orphan back and let them decide;
  don't assume the omission was a mistake.
