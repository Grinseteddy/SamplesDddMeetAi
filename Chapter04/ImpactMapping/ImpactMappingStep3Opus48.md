# Answer:

I read this against the impact-mapping skill, the brainstorm (Image 2), and the canvas (Image 3). Here's the critique — held as hypotheses for you to push back on, not verdicts.

## The map as one sentence

*"In order to reach 2,500 paying members in two years, a single / young family will find recipes, learn cooking, and follow cooks — and we'll support that with a Meal Chooser, Grandma AI, a Paywall, etc."*

It mostly parses, which is a good sign. But the one word that has to carry the goal — **paying** — is exactly the link the map leaves implicit. That's where I'd focus.

## Biggest risks first

**1. The whole map bets that engagement converts to payment, and never tests it.** The goal is *paying* members (good — that's an outcome, not a vanity "signups" metric). But almost every impact builds *free-tier* value — find recipes, learn cooking, follow cooks, shop with discount, publish a cookbook — on the unstated assumption that engaged free users become paying ones. The actual money behaviours ("convert to paid plan," "paid member renews," "see premium content") sit in a thin cluster, and their only deliverables — Paywall (effort 1), Member management (1) — are the *least* developed things on the board. Worse, your two most expensive bets, **Timer and Grandma/Grandpa AI (both effort 3)**, are engagement features sitting about as far from the payment moment as anything on the map. *Test cheaply:* before building either 3-pointer, what's the smallest experiment that tells you which feature a free user would actually pay to keep? If you can't name it, the conversion assumption is unvalidated and it's load-bearing for the entire goal. *What would stop me worrying:* a believable shortest path — one impact you think drives conversion, validated first.

**2. The goal is an acquisition target, but the map has no acquisition path.** 2,500 paying members in two years is fundamentally about getting people *in the door* and through a funnel. The canvas even lists **Marketing** as a cost line. Yet the map has no actor, impact, or deliverable for acquisition, referral, or onboarding — it optimises what members do *after* they've somehow arrived. *Worth asking:* who produces the 2,500, and through what behaviour? Right now the map silently assumes they appear.

**3. A second, unmeasured goal is smuggled in.** The canvas shows **two** revenue streams — *Monthly member fee* and *Ads by partners* — but the goal names only members. So "pay for ads" and "Ads management" are branches serving ad revenue with nothing in the goal to anchor them. Either widen the goal to include revenue, or these are strategically orphaned. (And the actor who "pays for ads" — an advertiser — isn't in the actor list at all.)

## Level-by-level notes

**Goal.** Strong on measurement, but it bundles the solution ("recipe sharing platform") into the centre. Minor — your operative metric is clean.

**Actors.** Segments (Single, Young family) and partners (Kitchen ware retailer, Grocery store, Photo book service, Chefs) are solid. But **"Prospects" and "Convertable" aren't actors — they're funnel states** of users you've already listed, which muddies the level. And there are **no obstructers**: the free-rider who never converts, the member who churns after month one, the app store as gatekeeper. Missing negative actors is where a paying-member plan gets blindsided.

**Impacts.** Several aren't behaviour changes: **"supports unexperienced members"** (that's *our* action, not an actor doing something) and **"Cookbooks make curious"** (the subject is cookbooks, not an actor) belong elsewhere. The real behaviour underneath each is missing.

**Deliverables.** Effort numbers are good — but they expose the problem in risk #1: the 3-pointers aren't on the path to the goal. Mostly one deliverable per impact, so few alternatives were explored.

## Against the brainstorm (Image 2)

Fidelity is reasonable — Grandma/Grandpa AI traces cleanly to "Ask grandma" / "Grandpa shows how to do it," and Nutrition management to the Allergenes/Dietary-requirements cluster. But note **what got dropped**, especially the highlighted (blue) ideas: *Voice control, Smart kitchen, Fridge-photo recipe generation, Food pairing, Leftover optimisation.* The fridge-photo and food-pairing ideas in particular were flagged as interesting on the board and vanished in synthesis. More tellingly, the brainstorm contains **almost no monetisation thinking at all** — so the riskiest part of your goal (making people pay) is the part the room never actually ideated on. The payment layer was added afterward, outside the group's exploration.

## Against the canvas (Image 3)

Good alignment on segments, partners, and value propositions (Easy-to-find recipes, Cooking support, Specialized nutrition, AI proposals, Planner all map through). Two gaps stand out: the **Marketing cost line has no counterpart in the map** (reinforcing risk #2), and the canvas resources **Cooks / Recipes** point at a two-sided cold-start problem — you need enough recipe supply before consumption (let alone payment) is worth anything — which the map doesn't surface as an assumption.

## What's strong (protect this)

A genuinely measured goal; clean segment-and-partner fidelity to the canvas; effort estimates that *make* prioritisation discussable; and AI features that trace back to real brainstorm ideas rather than being invented in synthesis.

**Path forward:** I'd pick the single impact you believe causes conversion, build the cheapest thing that could trigger it, and measure conversion against the goal *before* funding either effort-3 feature — and decide whether the goal should name revenue, not just members. If you can show me the conversion assumption has already been tested, risk #1 goes away and most of the rest is tidy-up.
