# Critique Techniques

The Devil's Advocate's working toolkit. Each technique includes when to reach
for it and a worked example. Pick the one or two that bite hardest for the idea
in front of you — don't run the whole list.

## Assumption mapping

Every plan rests on beliefs that, if false, sink it. Make them explicit, then
attack the shakiest load-bearing one.

- Ask: "What has to be true for this to work?" List the answers.
- Sort by *how confident are we* × *how badly does it hurt if wrong*.
- The high-impact / low-confidence cell is where to spend your challenge.

*Example.* Idea: a referral program to grow signups. Hidden assumptions: that
existing users like the product enough to recommend it; that their friends are
also in the target market; that the reward won't attract fraud. The weakest is
usually the first — challenge it: "What's our evidence current users would
actually refer us? If retention is shaky, a referral engine just pours people
into a leaky bucket."

## Premortem

The most reliable risk-finder. Project into a future where the idea has already
failed, then explain it.

- Prompt: "It's a year from now and this was a clear failure. Walk me through
  the most likely story of how it went wrong."
- The framing (failure as given) bypasses optimism and licenses people to name
  doubts they'd otherwise suppress.
- Collect the failure stories, then ask which are cheap to prevent now.

## Steelman the alternative

Build the *strongest* possible case for the rejected option, the opposite
approach, or doing nothing. This is the opposite of a strawman.

- "The best argument for *not* doing this is…"
- "If someone smart preferred the boring option, their reasoning would be…"
- If the favored idea can't clearly beat its steelmanned rival, that gap is the
  finding — surface it.

## Second-order effects ("and then what?")

The first-order outcome is usually fine; the damage hides downstream.

- "This works — and then what happens?" Repeat two or three hops out.
- Probe: who is harmed or excluded; what behavior it incentivizes; what breaks
  at 10× scale; what happens if it succeeds *too* well.

*Example.* "Auto-approving small refunds cuts support load — and then what?
Word spreads, abuse rises, and now you need fraud tooling you didn't budget for."

## Evidence and falsifiability

Move the conversation from conviction to test.

- "How would we *know* if we're wrong about this?"
- "What's the cheapest experiment that would tell us before we commit fully?"
- "What does the base rate say — how often do efforts like this actually work?"

## The bias checklist

Name the distortion operating in the room. Often, naming it *is* the
contribution — it gives the group permission to re-examine.

- **Anchoring / fixation:** locked onto the first idea; never explored distant
  alternatives. (The fixation that Paulus et al. flag as a core group failure.)
- **Groupthink:** apparent consensus because dissent feels unwelcome — not
  because the idea is sound.
- **Sunk cost:** defending the plan because of effort already spent.
- **Confirmation:** collecting only evidence that supports the favored idea.
- **Availability / recency:** over-weighting the most recent or vivid case.

## Inversion (Osborn's "reverse")

Borrow Osborn's idea-manipulation move and point it at risk.

- "How could we *guarantee* this fails?" Brainstorm the sabotage list.
- Then read each item back as a risk to design against.
- Surfaces failure modes that a straight "what are the risks?" misses.

## The smart-skeptic test

Compress everything into one question and lead with the answer.

- "What's the single strongest objection a sharp, fair outside critic would
  raise the moment they saw this?"
- Voice that one first, in full force, then offer what would neutralize it.

## Red-team question bank (grab-and-go)

- Who loses if this works?
- What are we optimizing that we'll regret optimizing?
- What's the assumption we're least willing to question — and why?
- If we had to kill this idea with one fact, what fact would we go looking for?
- What would have to be true for the opposite choice to be correct?
- Where are we treating a guess as a fact?
- What's the failure mode we're hoping no one brings up?
