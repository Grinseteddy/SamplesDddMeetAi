# Fit, Coherence, and Environment

Block-by-block critique catches weak parts. Most canvases that *look* complete
still fail on **fit between blocks**, on **coherence as a whole**, or on
**collision with the outside world**. This file covers all three. (Grounded in
Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010 — the building-block fit logic, the SWOT-style
assessment, and the four-forces "Business Model Environment.")

---

## A. Fit between blocks — where canvases really break

A business model is a system; the test is whether the blocks reinforce each
other. Trace these connections explicitly and challenge any that don't hold:

- **Value Proposition ↔ Customer Segment.** Does the value solve a problem *this
  specific segment* actually feels, strongly enough to act? A mismatch here
  invalidates everything downstream.
- **Channels ↔ Segment.** Do the chosen channels reach where this segment
  actually pays attention and buys? Right message, wrong pipe = no reach.
- **Customer Relationships ↔ Segment & Cost.** Does the relationship type match
  what the segment expects *and* what the cost structure can fund?
- **Revenue Streams ↔ Value Proposition.** Are customers paying for the thing
  that delivers the value? Is the payer the same as the beneficiary? (If not —
  e.g. a free user side and a paying side — both sides must be on the canvas.)
- **Key Resources/Activities/Partners ↔ Value Proposition.** Do the things on
  the left genuinely produce the value on the right? Is any *load-bearing*
  resource/activity missing?
- **Cost Structure ↔ Revenue Streams.** Do the costs implied by the left side
  come in under the revenue the right side generates — with margin, and with
  workable cash-flow *timing* (do you collect before or after you spend)?

A useful prompt: *"Pick the single connection this model most depends on. Now
what's the evidence it holds?"*

## B. The coherence / story test

A strong canvas tells **one** coherent story, with the Value Proposition linking
the customer-facing right side to the infrastructure-facing left side. Read it
back as a single sentence:

> "We help **[segment]** accomplish **[job/solve problem]** through
> **[channel]** and **[relationship]**, earning **[revenue stream]**, by
> deploying **[key resources/activities]** and **[partners]**, at
> **[cost structure]**."

If that sentence is contradictory, requires three different sentences (the
"two-businesses-pretending-to-be-one" problem), or has a blank where a clause
should be, that's a structural finding — usually more important than any single
weak block.

Coherence red flags:
- Premium value proposition + bargain cost structure (or the reverse).
- A growth story with no matching resource/cost to fuel the growth.
- An "efficiency" left side feeding a "high-touch" right side.

## C. Stress against the four environmental forces

A model can be internally coherent and still be roadkill for its context. Probe
the four forces (Osterwalder & Pigneur's Business Model Environment):

1. **Market forces** — Who else serves this need? What are the switching costs
   *toward* and *away from* us? How attractive are the revenues, really? Is the
   segment growing or shrinking?
2. **Industry forces** — Incumbents (will they retaliate or copy?), new
   entrants, substitute products/services, suppliers and value-chain actors, and
   other stakeholders (regulators, unions, investors).
3. **Key trends** — Technology, regulatory, societal/cultural, and socioeconomic
   trends. Does any trend make the model *more* fragile or obsolete on its
   intended timeline? Does the model ride a trend or fight one?
4. **Macroeconomic forces** — Global conditions, capital markets (can this be
   funded in the current climate?), commodity/resource prices, and economic
   infrastructure the model relies on.

Lead with whichever force most threatens *this* model rather than walking all
four mechanically.

## D. SWOT-style assessment lens (per area)

For a more systematic sweep, the book applies a SWOT assessment to the building
blocks. Useful framing questions:

- **Value Proposition:** Well aligned with customer needs? Strong network
  effects? Substitutes or strong complements? How does it stack against
  competitors?
- **Cost / Revenue:** Are revenues recurring and predictable? Diversified or
  concentrated? Sustainable margins? Do we collect before we spend? Are costs
  predictable and scaling sensibly?
- **Infrastructure (resources / activities / partners):** Can we rely on key
  resources, or is there disruption/supply risk? Is execution focus right (are
  we doing the right things in-house vs outsourced)? Are partner relationships
  sound and non-coercive?
- **Customer interface (segments / channels / relationships):** Churn rate? Is
  the base well segmented? Acquisition working? Channels efficient and effective?
  Strong relationships and switching costs that favor us? Brand strength?

Each question that gets a weak or "we don't know" answer is a candidate for the
"biggest risks" section of the critique — especially the *don't-knows*, which
point straight at the cheapest valuable experiment to run next.
