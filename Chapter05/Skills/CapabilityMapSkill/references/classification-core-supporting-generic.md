# Classification — Core, Supporting, Generic

The user wants every capability marked **core**, **supporting**, or **generic**.
This is the *competitive-contribution* lens — how much a capability differentiates
*this* business — and it is what turns a capability map into investment and
sourcing decisions. It is **not** the same as G211's strategic/core/supporting
*stratification tiers* (altitude/audience); see the warning at the end.

The classification is a judgement call, always relative to a *specific* strategy
and industry. Your job as critic is less to assert the "right" colour than to
test whether each marking is *defensible* and whether the marking *as a whole*
still carries information.

---

## The three classes (and what each implies)

### Core (differentiating)
The "secret sauce." A capability that creates competitive advantage — it drives
the value proposition or the North Star Metric, and a competitor cannot easily
copy or buy it. **Decision implication:** *invest, own, and keep building the
gap.* This is where money and the best people should concentrate.

**Strong core looks like:** something you do that customers notice and rivals
can't trivially reproduce — a recommendation engine for a content platform, a
logistics network for a same-day retailer, underwriting for a specialist insurer.

### Supporting (context)
Specific to this business or domain and *necessary* to deliver, but not a source
of advantage — parity with the field is enough. **Decision implication:**
*standardize and optimize for efficiency;* spend the minimum that keeps it at
par. There's usually a cliff beyond which extra investment yields nothing.

**Strong supporting looks like:** a capability you genuinely need and that has
some domain specificity, but where being *better* than rivals at it wouldn't win
you a single customer.

### Generic (commodity)
Common to almost every business, with no competitive advantage — "everybody does
this the same way." **Decision implication:** *buy, outsource, or standardize to
the lowest sustainable cost;* never build bespoke. Payroll, email, facilities,
and often CRM and basic accounting live here.

**Strong generic looks like:** something you could hand to a vendor tomorrow and
no customer would ever know or care.

> A clean way to hold the three: **core = where you must be *better*; supporting
> = where you must be *good enough*; generic = where you must be *cheap*.**

## A decision procedure (apply per capability)

1. **Necessity check.** Does the business need this at all? (If no, it may not
   belong on the map.) Necessity gets it onto the map — it does **not** make it
   core. Keep going.
2. **Advantage check.** If two competitors both had this capability at industry
   norm, would this firm still win on the strength of *this* capability? If
   yes → candidate **core**. If no → it's supporting or generic.
3. **Specificity check** (for the non-core ones). Is the capability specific to
   this business/domain (→ **supporting**) or generic across most businesses
   (→ **generic**)?
4. **Strategy tie-back.** For anything you called **core**, name the value
   proposition, NSM, or strategic objective it advances. *A core capability that
   ties to nothing is mis-marked* — re-mark it or surface the missing strategy.

---

## The common mis-markings — what to challenge

- **Necessity-as-core (the big one).** "We literally cannot operate without it,
  so it's core." Mission-critical ≠ differentiating. A CRM can be vital to a
  sales org and still be commodity, because every rival has the same one. Ask:
  *does being better at this than competitors win business, or just keep the
  lights on?*
- **Core inflation.** A third or half the map marked core. If everything is core,
  nothing is — the marking no longer guides investment, which was its only job.
  Push for a *small* core: "If you could only protect three capabilities from a
  budget cut, which three? Those are core; be sceptical of the rest."
- **Strategy- and industry-dependence ignored.** The same capability is core for
  one firm and generic for another. Low-latency execution is core for a
  high-frequency trading shop and near-irrelevant for a buy-and-hold fund. A
  marking copied from a reference model or a competitor, rather than derived from
  *this* firm's strategy, is unfalsifiable. Ask whose strategy the marking serves.
- **Context drift treated as fixed.** Supporting/generic can *become* core if the
  firm deliberately invests to differentiate there (and core can decay to
  commodity as the market catches up). Markings rot. A map with no sense of which
  capabilities are *moving* between classes is a snapshot mistaken for a strategy.
- **Generic that's secretly core.** A capability dismissed as commodity — and so
  starved or outsourced — that is actually where this firm could differentiate.
  The mirror image of necessity-as-core, and easy to miss because nobody's
  defending it. Cross-check against the value proposition and NSM.
- **Outsourcing the core.** A capability marked generic (and handed to a partner)
  that the strategy needs to be differentiating. The most expensive mistake on
  the list — see the Business Model Canvas cross-check.

## The falsifiable challenge to keep in your pocket

For any **core** marking: *"What can this business do with this capability that a
competitor buying the same tools and hiring the same people couldn't replicate in
a year? If the honest answer is 'nothing yet,' it's parity — supporting at best —
not core."* For any **generic** marking on a capability near the value
proposition: *"Is this really commodity for you, or have you just never tried to
win here?"*

---

## Don't confuse this with G211's stratification tiers

G211's worked example *stratifies* capabilities into **Strategic / Core /
Supporting** tiers — but that is an **altitude** classification (executive
direction-setting / customer-facing engine / behind-the-scenes), answering "which
stakeholder cares and where does it sit in the business." The **core / supporting
/ generic** marking here is a **competitive-contribution** classification,
answering "how much does it differentiate us, and so should we invest, standardize,
or outsource." A capability can be *Strategic*-tier by altitude yet *generic* by
contribution (e.g. board-level financial reporting), or *Supporting*-tier by
altitude yet *core* by contribution (a back-office algorithm nobody sees that
quietly wins the market). When a map or a person says "core," establish which
sense they mean before you critique the marking — conflating the two is itself a
finding worth surfacing.
