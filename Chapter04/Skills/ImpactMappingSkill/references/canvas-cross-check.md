# Map vs. Business Model Canvas — Cross-Check

This pass applies **only when the person gives you a Business Model Canvas** as
the source the impact map was derived from. Where a brainstorm is the *raw,
messy* source (see `collaboration-cross-check.md`), a canvas is the *structured,
strategic* source: it states who the business serves, what value it promises, how
it earns, and what it costs. A sound impact map should be a faithful,
goal-focused **projection** of that strategy — its actors, goal, impacts, and
deliverables should all trace back to something on the canvas. Where they don't,
either the map has drifted from the strategy or the strategy has a gap the map
exposed; both are findings worth surfacing.

The nine-block Business Model Canvas is from Osterwalder & Pigneur; van Kelle et
al. (2024) also treat it as one of the collaborative modeling tools a team uses
upstream of design. **Do not critique the canvas itself here** — that's a
separate exercise (the user has a dedicated canvas critic). Use the canvas only
as the reference against which you check the *map's* fidelity.

---

## Step A — Read the canvas and map its blocks to the four levels

The nine blocks don't sit one-to-one under the four impact-map levels, but they
project onto them predictably. Note what each block implies:

| Canvas block | Projects onto the impact map as… |
|---|---|
| **Customer Segments** | candidate **Actors** (WHO) — each served segment is an actor whose behaviour you may need to change |
| **Key Partners** | candidate **Actors** — enablers, and potential *obstructers/gatekeepers* |
| **Value Propositions** | the **Goal** (WHY) and the desired **Impacts** (HOW) — the behaviour change that makes the value real |
| **Revenue Streams** | the **Goal** — the map's objective should ultimately move a revenue stream or strategic objective |
| **Channels** & **Customer Relationships** | **Impacts** and **Deliverables** — the routes through which actor behaviour changes |
| **Key Resources / Key Activities** | feasibility of **Deliverables**, and internal **Actors** who deliver |
| **Cost Structure** | a reality check on the **Deliverables** — what they imply you'll spend |

Build this mapping before comparing, so each finding can point to a specific
block.

---

## Step B — The catalogue of map-vs-canvas findings

### Dropped segment or partner
A Customer Segment or Key Partner central to the canvas that the map never turns
into an **actor** — especially a *paying* segment or a gatekeeper/partner who
could block delivery. The map is then silently betting it can hit the goal
without changing the behaviour of someone the business depends on.

### Goal–strategy mismatch
The map's **goal** doesn't advance any Value Proposition or Revenue Stream on the
canvas — the map is optimising something the business model doesn't actually
monetise or value. Or the goal targets a part of the canvas that isn't the
constraint (polishing a healthy revenue stream while a fragile one goes
unaddressed). Either the map aimed at the wrong thing, or the canvas is missing
the objective the map assumes — ask which.

### Value not realised by the impacts
The **impacts** (behaviour changes) wouldn't actually deliver the Value
Proposition the canvas promises that segment — a behaviour change that doesn't
connect to the value the customer is paying for.

### Deliverables that ignore the cost/resource side
A **deliverable** silently assumes Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partners, or
costs that the canvas never accounted for — a hidden bet on the expensive,
left-hand side of the business model. Or the deliverables contradict the Cost
Structure (an elaborate build on a lean, cost-driven model). This is where an
impact map that looks clean on the value side quietly breaks the economics.

### Channel / relationship mismatch
A deliverable assumes a route to the actor (a channel, a relationship type) that
the canvas's Channels / Customer Relationships don't support — e.g. a
self-service feature for a segment the canvas serves through high-touch sales.

### Whole canvas region unrepresented
An entire side of the canvas — frequently the whole infrastructure/cost half
(Key Resources, Activities, Partners, Cost Structure) — has no representation in
the map's actors or deliverables, meaning feasibility and cost were never
considered when the branches were chosen.

---

## Step C — Report it as a question, concretely

Tie every finding to a specific block ("the canvas lists two paying segments;
only one appears as an actor"). And hold it as a hypothesis, not a verdict: a
mismatch between map and canvas could mean *either* artifact is wrong, and it's
the team's call which to fix. A projection *should* simplify the strategy; your
job is to ask whether it dropped or contradicted the part that mattered, then let
them decide.
