# Nine Building Blocks — Critique Question Bank

For each block: the core diagnostic question (from Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010),
what a *strong* block looks like, and the common holes to probe. Pick the
questions that bite hardest for the specific canvas — this is a menu, not a
script to read aloud.

---

## 1. Customer Segments (right side)

**Diagnostic:** For whom are we creating value? Who are the most important
customers? *(Mass market, niche, segmented, diversified, multi-sided platform.)*

**Strong looks like:** a segment specific enough that you could name three real
people in it and describe their day.

**Common holes:**
- "Everyone" is not a segment. Over-broad targeting hides the fact that no one
  in particular has been understood.
- Multiple distinct segments quietly bundled as one, each needing a *different*
  value prop / channel / relationship — the canvas should arguably be split.
- A multi-sided platform where only one side is described (e.g. the users but
  not the advertisers who actually pay).
- The segment is described demographically ("women 25–40") but not by the *job
  they're trying to get done* or the problem they feel.
- No evidence the segment exists at the size assumed.

---

## 2. Value Propositions (center)

**Diagnostic:** What value do we deliver? Which problem are we solving, which
need are we satisfying, for which segment? *(Value can come from: newness,
performance, customization, "getting the job done," design, brand/status, price,
cost reduction, risk reduction, accessibility, convenience/usability.)*

**Strong looks like:** a specific pain it removes or gain it creates, that the
named segment would actually pay or switch for.

**Common holes:**
- A feature list masquerading as a value proposition ("AI-powered, cloud-based")
  with no stated customer pain relieved.
- "Better/faster/cheaper" with no reference point — better than *what*, by how
  much, noticed by whom?
- Value that is real but not *differentiated* — easily copied, or already
  offered by an incumbent the customer is happy with.
- Value the founder cares about that the segment doesn't (a solution hunting for
  a problem).
- No answer to "why now / why hasn't this been done?"

---

## 3. Channels (right side)

**Diagnostic:** Through which channels do our segments want to be reached? Which
work best, which are most cost-efficient? *(Covers five phases: awareness,
evaluation, purchase, delivery, after-sales. Own vs partner; direct vs indirect.)*

**Strong looks like:** a believable path from "never heard of us" to "bought and
supported," matched to where the segment actually spends attention.

**Common holes:**
- Only the *purchase* phase is addressed; awareness ("how will anyone find
  out?") and after-sales are blank.
- Assumes a cheap viral / organic channel with no mechanism for why it spreads.
- Channel doesn't match the segment (enterprise buyers via TikTok ads; teenagers
  via cold outreach).
- Heavy dependence on a partner channel that also controls the customer
  relationship and could disintermediate you.
- Channel economics ignored — cost to acquire through it vs. customer value.

---

## 4. Customer Relationships (right side)

**Diagnostic:** What type of relationship does each segment expect? *(Personal
assistance, dedicated personal assistance, self-service, automated service,
communities, co-creation.)* Driven by acquisition, retention, or upselling?

**Strong looks like:** a relationship type whose cost fits the revenue, and that
the segment actually wants.

**Common holes:**
- Implicit assumption of cheap self-service for a segment that expects
  hand-holding (or vice versa: white-glove service on a low-margin product).
- No retention story — all acquisition, nothing about why customers stay.
- Churn ignored. A leaky bucket can sink an otherwise fine model.
- Relationship type contradicts the cost structure (high-touch support promised
  on a "lean, automated" cost base).

---

## 5. Revenue Streams (right side)

**Diagnostic:** For what value are customers *really* willing to pay? What do
they pay for now, how, and how would they prefer to? *(Types: asset sale, usage
fee, subscription, lending/renting/leasing, licensing, brokerage, advertising.
Pricing: fixed vs dynamic.)*

**Strong looks like:** the people who *receive* the value are the people who
*pay*, via a mechanism they'll accept, at a price that clears costs.

**Common holes:**
- The classic free-product gap: value goes to users, but the revenue line is a
  hopeful "ads later" with no advertiser segment on the canvas.
- "Willing to pay" assumed, never tested. Stated interest ≠ a credit card.
- Single revenue stream / single big customer = concentration risk.
- Pricing mechanism leaves money on the table or is mismatched to the buying
  behavior (one-time sale where a subscription fits, or vice versa).
- Revenue recognized far in the future while costs are immediate — cash-flow
  timing buried.

---

## 6. Key Resources (left side)

**Diagnostic:** What key resources do the value proposition, channels,
relationships, and revenue streams require? *(Physical, intellectual — brands,
patents, data — human, financial.)*

**Strong looks like:** the few resources without which the value proposition
literally cannot be delivered, and a credible way to get/keep them.

**Common holes:**
- A critical resource is assumed available (key talent, proprietary data, a
  license, capital) with no acquisition plan.
- The actual moat (data, network, IP) isn't listed as a resource at all.
- Resource needs that don't scale with the revenue model.
- Reliance on a single founder / single point of failure.

---

## 7. Key Activities (left side)

**Diagnostic:** What key activities does the value proposition require? *(Three
archetypes: production, problem-solving, platform/network.)*

**Strong looks like:** the handful of things the business must be excellent at,
consistent with the value proposition type.

**Common holes:**
- Everything listed as key, so nothing is — no prioritization.
- The genuinely hard activity is glossed (e.g. "we'll do customer support" for a
  model whose whole value is support quality).
- Activity type mismatched to value type (a platform model with no
  platform-building activity).

---

## 8. Key Partnerships (left side)

**Diagnostic:** Who are our key partners and suppliers? Which resources/
activities do we get from them? *(Motivations: optimization & economies of
scale, reduction of risk/uncertainty, acquisition of resources/activities.
Types: strategic alliance, coopetition, joint venture, buyer–supplier.)*

**Strong looks like:** partners chosen for a clear motivation, with the
dependency understood.

**Common holes:**
- A "partner" who is really a competitor, gatekeeper, or platform that can
  change terms or cut you off (channel/platform dependency risk).
- Critical capability outsourced that should be owned (handing the moat to a
  supplier).
- Partnership assumed but no reason given for *why the partner says yes*.
- No partner where one is clearly needed (a regulated activity with no
  licensed/compliance partner).

---

## 9. Cost Structure (left side)

**Diagnostic:** What are the most important costs? Which resources/activities are
most expensive? *(Cost-driven vs value-driven. Characteristics: fixed costs,
variable costs, economies of scale, economies of scope.)*

**Strong looks like:** the big cost lines named, tied to the resources/activities
above them, and clearly smaller than the revenue they enable.

**Common holes:**
- Customer-acquisition cost omitted — often the dominant real cost.
- Fixed vs variable split unexamined, so the path to a margin at scale is
  unclear.
- Cost base contradicts the positioning (a value-driven luxury proposition on a
  cost-driven, no-frills cost structure, or the reverse).
- Hidden costs of the chosen relationship/channel (support, returns, compliance).
- No version of the unit economics: does one customer pay for itself?
