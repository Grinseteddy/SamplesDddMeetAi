Answer:

I worked through the impact map on its own logic first, then checked it against the brainstorm and the canvas. Here's what I found.

## The map as one sentence

*"In order to reach 2,500 members in two years, our actors (singles, young families, cooks, plus retail/chef partners) will find recipes easily, cook with support, shop, learn, and publish — and we'll build search, AI, ordering, a planner, ratings, and publishing to support that."*

It reads coherently and every branch connects. But it has one structural fault line that I think outweighs everything else, so I'll start there.

## Biggest risks

**1. The map builds for retention, but the goal measures acquisition — and the growth levers got dropped.**
Your goal is a *member count*: 2,500 sign-ups. Yet almost every impact on the map improves life for people who are *already inside* — find recipes easily, cooking support, budget cooking, learn cooking. Those drive engagement and retention. Very little on the map drives *getting to* 2,500 people in the first place. The obvious growth engine for a "sharing platform" is the sharing loop itself (members share → new members arrive), and it's barely present. Tellingly, the brainstorm had the growth levers — Sharing on Instagram, Followers, Sponsor contest, social login — and the canvas explicitly lists *Marketing* as a cost. None of that reached the map. So the map could be fully delivered and still miss its number.
*How to test:* for each impact, ask "does this win a *new* member, or retain an existing one?" If the acquisition column is nearly empty, the map isn't serving its goal. *What would resolve it:* add at least one actor/impact branch about acquisition (referral, social sharing, marketing) — or change the goal to a retention/engagement metric if that's what you actually care about.

**2. The goal is a proxy that's disconnected from the revenue model.**
"2,500 members" is measurable (metric + target + deadline — genuinely good), but the canvas earns money via *monthly member fee* and *ads by partners*. "Members" doesn't distinguish paying from free, or active from dormant. You could hit 2,500 sign-ups and still be unviable, and the map has no impact about converting to paying or willingness to pay. *What would resolve it:* sharpen the goal to the thing that keeps the lights on (e.g. "2,500 *paying* members" or a revenue target), so the map is anchored to viability, not a vanity count.

**3. A stated value proposition has no line of sight on the map.**
The canvas promises *"Recipes for specialized nutrition requirements"* and the brainstorm raised it concretely (Allergenes, Dietary requirements, even fridge-photo recipe generation). The map has none of it — no allergen/dietary impact or deliverable. That's a promised value with nothing built to deliver it: the feature-factory failure running in reverse.

## Level-by-level notes

- **Goal** — well-measured, but see risks 1 & 2. Also "recipe sharing platform" bakes a *solution* into the goal statement; the brainstorm and canvas show something much broader (shopping, AI, planning, e-commerce), so the framing undersells the product.
- **Actors** — partners map cleanly, but there's no **creator/author actor**, which is odd for a *sharing* platform whose canvas relationship is literally "weekly for creating." Who supplies the recipes? Also no **obstructing actor** — no competitor, no churning user, nobody who could block 2,500.
- **Impacts** — mostly genuine behaviour changes, except *"Provide cooking support,"* which is written from the system's side, not the actor's. Reframe as the user's behaviour ("cooks unfamiliar dishes successfully"). No impacts to *avoid* (e.g. overwhelming beginners, recipe spam).
- **Deliverables** — twelve deliverables across seven impacts, with almost no pruning from a very large brainstorm. They read as a fixed scope, not as options/bets, and there's no **shortest path** flagged — everything looks equally weighted (the one emphasised box, "Find easily recipes," is a retention impact, not a growth one). For a small team chasing 2,500 members, this is boiling the ocean.

## Map vs. its sources

**Against the brainstorm — dropped ideas worth a second look:**
- **Larder tracker** — the *namesake* pantry feature, flagged as a special idea, is absent from the map entirely.
- The whole **growth/community side**: Followers, Sharing on Instagram, Sponsor contest, social login (Authentication) — gone, which compounds risk #1.
- **Creator mechanics**: Cooking Videos, Making Photos, Remix of recipes — collapsed into a single "Content" sticky.
- Most of the **flagged (blue) ideas** — Voice control, Smart kitchen, Allergenes, Fridge-photo generation, Food pairing — didn't survive. When the *highlighted* ideas are the ones that vanish, that's a sign of premature convergence on the safe, generic features. Worth confirming those drops were deliberate, not attrition.

**Against the canvas:**
- Segments (Singles, Young families) and Key Partners (kitchenware, grocery, photo book, chefs) trace through cleanly — good fidelity. ✓
- *"Specialized nutrition"* value prop dropped (risk #3).
- **Marketing** (a flagged cost on the canvas) has no representation anywhere in the map — again the acquisition gap.
- The pile of AI deliverables (AI proposals, Grandma/Grandpa AI, Meal Chooser) leans hard on "AI search support" + cloud + licenses. The canvas names those costs but the *volume* of AI features here may be a heavier bet than a lean cost structure assumes.

## What's strong (protect it)

The goal is actually measured — metric, target, and deadline — which most maps fail to do. The partner-actors and the two customer segments are consistent across all three artifacts, and the core "find recipes / cooking support" thread is coherent end to end. The bones are good.

## The one move I'd make first

Add an acquisition branch and decide the shortest path: pick the *one or two* impacts most likely to move members toward 2,500 (almost certainly a sharing/referral loop plus the differentiated "specialized nutrition" hook), and demote the rest to "later." That single edit closes the goal-vs-map gap and turns a build-everything list back into a set of bets you can prune.

These are all hypotheses — if any of them rest on a decision you already made and discarded for a good reason (say, growth is handled outside this map), tell me and I'll happily drop it. Which of the three risks do you want to dig into?
