# Answer:

Alright — Devil's Advocate hat on. I'll group what's on the board so we can see the shape of it, then attack each part. Don't take any of this personally; that's the job.

Here's how the ~35 notes actually cluster:

- **Recipe core:** Store recipes, Remix of recipes, Import handwritten notes, Dietary requirements, the three photo notes (Making / Presentation / Ingredients with Photos)
- **Search & discovery:** Search with things I already have, Search with cooking time, Meal Chooser, Rating
- **In-kitchen tools:** Timer, Step-by-step cooking mode, Kitchen tools, Shopping Lists, Cooking Calendar, Leftover optimizations, Budget, Necessary stuff, Order ingredients directly
- **Social / community:** Cooking Club, Followers, Comments, Sharing on Instagram, Login with social account, Cookbook publishing, Cooking skill level
- **Content & people:** Cooking Videos, Ask grandma, Grandpa shows how to do it, Become a dessert chef, Grill master, Cooking skill learning
- **Business:** Sponsor contest, Famous chefs as partner

Now the objections.

**You've drawn five different products, not one.** This board is simultaneously a recipe app, a grocery-delivery service, a YouTube competitor, a social network, and an e-learning platform. Each of those is a company in its own right. If the team builds a little of all five, you ship five mediocre things and lose to whoever does any one of them well. What is the *one* sentence that says what this is?

**Most of it is table stakes, not a wedge.** Ratings, comments, shopping lists, timers, search, social login — every incumbent (Allrecipes, Yummly, Cookpad, Paprika, Tasty) already has these. Nothing here makes a user *switch*. The only notes that smell like genuine differentiation are "Search with things I already have," "Leftover optimizations," and "Remix of recipes." Everything else is parity work. Why would anyone leave their current app for this?

**The social features assume a community you don't have on day one.** Followers, Comments, Cooking Club, Cookbook publishing, Sponsor contest, Famous chefs as partner — all of these are worthless with zero users, and they're the *reason* you'd have zero users (classic two-sided cold start). What's the single-player value that's useful when I'm the only person on the platform? If there isn't one, the network never starts.

**"Sharing on Instagram" actively works against you.** You'd spend engineering effort building a feature whose entire purpose is to send your users' best content *to Instagram* instead of keeping it on your platform. You're subsidizing a competitor's engagement. Why?

**Three of these notes are operational sinkholes disguised as features.** "Order ingredients directly" means grocery logistics, regional inventory, razor-thin margins, and delivery partnerships — that's a separate business with its own ops team. "Cooking Videos" means hosting, storage, transcoding, and content moderation costs, competing head-on with TikTok and YouTube. "Import handwritten notes" is an OCR research problem for a niche use case. Any one of these could eat your whole runway.

**"Famous chefs as partner" is backwards.** Why would a famous chef join an unproven platform with no audience? You need the audience to attract the chefs, and you're listing the chefs as the way to get the audience. Which comes first, and who pays for it?

**Several notes aren't features at all — they're vibes.** "Become a dessert chef," "Grill master," "Cooking skill learning" — what do these *do*? A label isn't a feature. Until someone can describe the actual screen and what happens when you tap it, these are just nice words on yellow paper.

**You have obvious redundancy.** Making Photos / Presentation Photos / Ingredients with Photos = "support images," that's one note. Cooking skill level / Cooking skill learning overlap. Ask grandma / Grandpa shows how to do it / Cooking Videos are the same idea wearing three hats. The board looks fuller than the idea actually is.

**And the elephant: how does this make money?** Only two notes (Sponsor contest, Famous chefs) even gesture at revenue, and both depend on scale you don't have. "Budget" appears to be a *user* feature (cooking cheaply), which makes them spend *less*. What's the business model, and does any feature here support it?

So the three questions I'd force the team to answer before adding one more sticky note: **Who is the one user, and what do they get on a platform with no one else on it? What can we do that Allrecipes/TikTok can't or won't? And which of these notes do we *delete* today?**
